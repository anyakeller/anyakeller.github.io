A Not-so-brief Intro

Beginning with education, I graduated from Stuyvesant High School in New York City in 2017. I look back at my time at Stuyvesant as one of the most influential and inspirational parts of my life. I thoroughly enjoyed my physics class and AP physics class, math, english, social studies, and most of all, the two and a half years of computer science classes I took -- three semesters of which I opted in for out of my own interest. As my primary extracarricular, I was a member of the school's FRC robotics team. On the team I was a machinist for 4 years, two of which I held the position of primary machinist ( The duties of the primary machinist included the maintinence of the mills and lathes, directing and organizing the work of the other machinists, and maufaturing parts out of aluminum, steel, and various plastics). Due to my experience on the robotics team, I chose to major in mechanical engineering at Boston University. Midway through the first semester of my second yer I realized I wasn't reaching my full intellectual potential and it was the logical decision to take time off of school to work and improve myself.

Since I left school, I've worked as a machinist, a camp counselor, and currently, a mechanical engineering intern. Additionally, I'm taking a Full Stack Bootcamp course at Columbia University which I enjoy immensely.

This begs the question, Why am I taking a CS course if I'm a mechanical engineer? I've always loved CS in addition to the machining work I've done. Since I worked in the mechanichal engineering division of my robotics team, I was under the impression that mech E was my field. Once I took time off, I started thinking about why I chose mech E and I came to a realization, my primary skills in the mech E department included Computed Aided Design (CAD, 3D modeling), Computer Aided Manufacturing (writing toolpaths to generate code for machines), and metal/wood/plastic manufacturing. The key point was that I specialized in computer-related parts of mech E which related to design and artistic ability. I've always had an affinity for problem solving and it hit me, througout high school, in health class, I would code or CAD, not design mechanisms. At home I'd work on coding problems for fun, not solder wires (although I would if I had a soldering iron becase it's fun).

I'd love to be able to combine my mech E and computer science backgrounds to build robots. The process of designing, manufacturing, assembling, coding, and testing to see your project come to life is thrilling. Still, one has to start somewhere and with a bit of mech E under my belt, it's time to brush up on my code.
